# Rigid Body Kinematics & Lie Groups

## 1. Frame-Specific Representations

Points and vectors are described by coordinates that are only meaningful with respect to a corresponding coordinate frame.

## 2. Rotation Matrices and $SO(3)$

A rotation matrix mathematically defines how one coordinate frame is oriented relative to another. The set of all valid 3D rotation matrices forms the **Special Orthogonal Group**, denoted as **$SO(3)$**.

**Definition:** Given a reference coordinate frame A and a frame B, the rotation matrix $R_{ab} \in SO(3)$ is constructed as:

$$R_{ab} = \begin{bmatrix} x_{ab} & y_{ab} & z_{ab} \end{bmatrix}$$

**Properties of $SO(3)$:**

Any valid rotation matrix $R$ must satisfy two fundamental properties:

1. **Orthonormality:** The columns of $R$ are mutually orthonormal: $RR^T = R^TR = I$.
2. **Right-Handedness:** It preserves a right-handed coordinate system: $\det(R) = +1$.

## 3. The Calculus of Rotations: Deriving $so(3)$

To understand how rotations evolve over time, we must look at the derivative of a rotation matrix. In pure rotation, the motion of a point solely depends on $R(t)$.

**Step 1: Position Relationship**

The current position $p(t)$ of a point is its initial position $p(0)$ rotated over time:

$$p(t) = R(t) p(0)$$

**Step 2: Velocity Derivation**

Taking the time derivative yields:

$$\dot{p}(t) = \dot{R}(t) p(0)$$

**Step 3: Eliminating the Initial State**

Since $p(0) = R(t)^T p(t)$ (from the orthogonality property), we substitute it back:

$$\dot{p}(t) = \left( \dot{R}(t) R(t)^T \right) p(t)$$

**Step 4: Defining Angular Velocity**

The term $\dot{R}R^T$ evaluates to a $3 \times 3$ skew-symmetric matrix. This matrix space is the **Lie Algebra $so(3)$**, denoted by the hat operator $\hat{\omega}$:

$$\dot{p} = \hat{\omega} p \quad \text{(which is equivalent to the cross product } \omega \times p \text{)}$$

> **Physical Intuition:** The linear velocity $\dot{p}$ generated by a pure rotation is always perpendicular to both the rotation axis $\omega$ and the position vector $p$.

## 4. Exponential Mapping and Rodrigues' Formula

Instead of composing elemental rotation matrices, we can express a general rotation by integrating the velocity differential equation $\dot{p} = \hat{\omega}p$. The solution is the **Matrix Exponential**:

$$R = e^{\hat{\omega}\theta}$$

For a rotation by angle $\theta$ around an arbitrary unit axis $\omega$ ($||\omega|| = 1$), the Taylor series expansion of $e^{\hat{\omega}\theta}$ simplifies beautifully into **Rodrigues' Formula**:

$$R = I + \hat{\omega}\sin\theta + \hat{\omega}^2(1 - \cos\theta)$$

Here, the hat operator transforms the axis vector $\omega$ into its corresponding skew-symmetric matrix $\hat{\omega} \in so(3)$:

$$\omega = \begin{bmatrix} \omega_1 \\ \omega_2 \\ \omega_3 \end{bmatrix} \implies \hat{\omega} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -\omega_3 & \omega_2 \\ \omega_3 & 0 & -\omega_1 \\ -\omega_2 & \omega_1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

## 5. Rigid Body Transformations and $SE(3)$

Now, the object is not only rotating but also translating. We describe this full pose using a $4 \times 4$ homogeneous transformation matrix $g$. This belongs to the **Special Euclidean Group**, denoted as **$SE(3)$**.

$$g(t) = \begin{bmatrix} R(t) & d(t) \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

## 6. The Calculus of Rigid Motion: Deriving Twists ($se(3)$)

Just as we derived angular velocity for $SO(3)$, we can derive the full spatial velocity for $SE(3)$.

**Step 1: Homogeneous Position**

$$\begin{bmatrix} p(t) \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} R(t) & d(t) \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}}_{g(t)} \begin{bmatrix} p(0) \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Step 2: Velocity Derivation**

Following the exact same logic as $SO(3)$:

$$\begin{bmatrix} \dot{p} \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} = \dot{g} \begin{bmatrix} p(0) \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \left( \dot{g} g^{-1} \right) \begin{bmatrix} p \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Step 3: Deconstructing the Twist $\hat{\xi}$**

The result of $\dot{g} g^{-1}$ is a $4 \times 4$ matrix representing the spatial velocity, known as a **Twist**. This belongs to the **Lie Algebra $se(3)$**:

$$\hat{\xi} = \dot{g}g^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} \dot{R}R^T & \dot{d} - \dot{R}R^T d \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \hat{\omega} & v \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

> **CRITICAL INSIGHT:** The vector $v$ in the twist is **not** simply the translational velocity of the center of mass $\dot{d}$. Instead, it is:
>
> $$v = \dot{d} - \hat{\omega} d$$
>
> It represents the velocity of a point currently passing through the origin, compensating for the linear velocity induced by the rotation.

## 7. Adjoint Transformations (Changing Velocity Frames)

To solve the problem: *What does the same physical motion look like from a different coordinate frame?*

If you observe a twist $\hat{\xi}_b$ in frame B, and you want to know what this exact same velocity looks like in frame A, you apply a **Similarity Transformation** (the "Sandwich" matrix multiplication):

$$\hat{\xi}_a = g \hat{\xi}_b g^{-1}$$

### The Ultimate Difference: $SO(3)$ vs. $SE(3)$

When we expand this sandwich multiplication into a $6 \times 6$ **Adjoint Matrix** ($Ad_g$), the fundamental difference between pure rotation and full rigid body motion is exposed:

- **In $SO(3)$ (Pure Rotation):** The transformation of angular velocity is pure and simple:

  $$\omega_a = R \omega_b$$

  You merely changed your viewing angle; the spin's magnitude and relative direction remain intact.

- **In $SE(3)$ (Rigid Body Pose):** The transformation of linear velocity gains a cross-product term:

  $$v_a = R v_b + \omega_a \times p$$

  > **Physical Meaning (The Lever Arm Effect):** Rotation *contributes* to linear velocity. If your observation frame is exactly on the axis of rotation ($p=0$), you feel no induced linear velocity. However, if your observation frame is 3 meters away from the axis ($p=3$), even a minuscule angular velocity $\omega$ will generate a massive perceived linear velocity ($v$) due to the lever arm effect.
